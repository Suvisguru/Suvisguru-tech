Lesson 02 · the foundation under Kubernetes

Virtualization vs Containerization

Kubernetes runs containers. So before we go any further, let's see what a container actually is — and how it differs from the older idea, the virtual machine.

🚨 The 3 AM Nightmare

Your team needs to ship 60 microservices. Each one runs in its own virtual machine. Each VM carries a full operating system — that's 60 copies of Linux, hogging RAM and disk. Booting one takes minutes. Cold starts during traffic spikes are the difference between a sale and a lost customer. This lesson is about the lighter alternative — and why Kubernetes was built on top of it.

🎯 If you remember nothing else: VMs are houses (own everything, heavy). Containers are apartments (shared building, light).

Section 1 · Concept

What virtualization and containerization actually are

A virtual machine (VM) is a complete pretend computer running inside a real one. It has its own operating system, its own kernel, its own simulated disk, its own simulated network — everything a real computer has, just simulated by software. The software that creates and manages VMs is called a hypervisor. One physical server might run 5 to 50 VMs side by side.

A container is much lighter. It's a single isolated process that shares the host computer's operating system. It packages your app and the files your app needs, but it doesn't carry its own OS — it borrows the one running underneath. The software that creates and manages containers is called a container runtime (containerd, Docker, CRI-O, runc). One physical server might run hundreds or even thousands of containers.

So both are ways to run multiple apps on one computer with isolation between them. The difference is how much they carry around. A VM carries a whole OS. A container carries just the app. That's it. That single difference is what gives containers their speed, their density, and their portability — and it's why Kubernetes, which has to schedule lots of small things very quickly, picked containers as its unit.

Same hardware. Same isolation goal. Different amount of stuff carried per app.

A VM image is typically 5–20 GB. A container image is typically 10–500 MB. Why the gap?

Answer: b. The OS-shaped chunk is what's missing.

Section 2 · Before & After

Why containers showed up after VMs

VMs solved a real problem in the early 2000s: companies had servers running at 5–10% utilisation each, and turning every app into a VM let them pack many apps onto fewer servers. But by the late 2010s, even VMs felt heavy. Containers came along to take the same idea further.

Same hardware. VMs let you fit a few apps. Containers let you fit many.
Before · VMs only

One physical server might run 3 to 10 VMs. Each VM carried a full operating system, so you spent gigabytes of RAM and disk on the OS layer alone. Booting a VM took minutes — the OS had to start up just like a real computer.

Distributing an app meant shipping a VM image, often 5–20 GB. Updating it meant rebuilding the VM. Density was bounded by RAM spent on guest OSes, not by the app itself.

After · Containers

One physical server might run hundreds of containers. Each container shares the host's OS, so the only thing in the container is your app and its files — usually 10–500 MB. Starting a container takes milliseconds — there's no OS to boot.

Distributing an app means pushing a container image to a registry — a few hundred MB at most. Updating is a new image. Density is bounded by what your app actually needs, not by OS overhead.

What didn't change: the physical hardware underneath. Both VMs and containers ultimately run on real CPUs, real RAM, real disks. They just slice the hardware differently.

Section 3 · Analogy

VMs are houses. Containers are apartments.

Imagine you're putting up a new neighbourhood. You have two ways to house a hundred families. Option A: build a hundred separate houses, each on its own plot, each with its own roof, foundation, water heater, furnace, and electrical panel. Every family is fully self-contained. If one house catches fire, the others are completely safe — but you used a lot of land and built a lot of roofs and water heaters.

Option B: build one apartment building with a hundred units. Every family has their own walls, their own door, their own kitchen. But all the units share the building's foundation, roof, central heating, plumbing, electricity. You need way less land. Construction is faster. Maintenance of shared things happens once, not a hundred times. The trade-off: a problem in the shared infrastructure (a fire in the basement, a burst water main) affects everyone.

VMs are houses. Containers are apartments. The shared infrastructure of an apartment building is the host's operating system kernel.

A house has its own everything; an apartment shares the building. Same with VMs vs containers.
In the story……in Kubernetes

Each apartmentOne container

Section 4 · Two-level explanation

Two ways to hear it

Pick the level that fits where you are. Same idea, just at different depths.

ELI5 · explain like I'm 5

A virtual machine is like a tiny pretend computer inside a big computer. Each pretend computer has its own everything, just like a real one. A container is more like a little room inside a big room — it has just enough walls to keep things separate, but it shares the floor and the lights and the air with all the other little rooms.

ELI10 · explain like I'm 10

A VM is a complete simulated computer. The hypervisor (the program that creates VMs) tricks each VM into thinking it has its own CPU, RAM, disk, and operating system. Each VM boots up like a real computer because, to itself, it is one. A container takes a much smaller approach: it shares the host computer's operating system, but uses Linux features called namespaces and cgroups to give each container its own private view of files, processes, and network. Same kernel underneath, different views on top. That's why containers are tiny and start instantly.

⏸ Pause and check

A bank wants to run apps for three competing customers on the same hardware. The customers don't trust each other. VMs or containers?

Answer: b. Plain containers share the host kernel. A kernel exploit in one customer's container could reach others. VMs give each customer their own kernel — much stronger isolation.

Section 5 · Real-world scenarios

When each one is the right pick

Both are still used in the real world, often together. Here are four situations where the choice is clear.

A company keeping a 2008-era app alive (legacy workload)

An accounting system originally built for Windows Server 2008 still runs the back office. The company has no plan to rewrite it. The cleanest answer is a VM — the VM gives the app its own Windows Server 2008 environment, and the rest of the company moves on with modern infrastructure. Containers wouldn't help here: the app needs its specific OS.

A SaaS startup running 60 microservices

The team rewrote their app as 60 small services that need to start fast, scale up and down through the day, and ship updates several times per hour. Containers are the obvious choice — each service ships as a small container image, starts in under a second, and packs densely onto a few VMs (yes, the containers run inside VMs from the cloud provider — they're complementary, not opposed).

A multi-tenant SaaS with hostile tenants

A company runs apps on behalf of customers who don't trust each other (different banks, different government agencies, different competing brands). They need stronger isolation than "shared kernel with namespaces." VMs (or specialised sandboxed runtimes like gVisor or Kata) give each tenant their own kernel — a kernel exploit in one tenant can't reach the others. Container isolation alone isn't strong enough.

A CI/CD system running 50,000 test jobs per day

Every commit triggers tests across many languages. The team needs each test job to start in seconds, run an isolated environment, and tear down. Containers nail this — spin up, run, tear down, repeat, with almost no overhead. A VM-based pipeline would burn most of its time waiting for VMs to boot.

Section 7 · Misconceptions, flashcards & quiz

Lock it in

Three common misconceptions to clear up first, then the flashcards and quiz.

Common Misconceptions

Myth: A container has its own operating system.
Truth: It shares the host's kernel. The "OS-like" feel is a private view, not a real second OS.
Myth: Containers replaced VMs.
Truth: They're complementary. Most cloud Kubernetes runs containers inside VMs the cloud provides.
Myth: Containers are unsafe because they share a kernel.
Truth: They're safe enough for trusting tenants. For hostile multi-tenancy, use VMs or sandboxed runtimes (gVisor, Kata).

What's a hypervisor?

The software that creates and manages virtual machines. Examples: VMware ESXi, Microsoft Hyper-V, KVM, Xen. Sits between the hardware and the VMs.
What's a container runtime?
tap to flip
The software that creates and manages containers. Examples: containerd, CRI-O, runc. Sits on top of the host OS and uses kernel features to isolate containers.
Biggest size difference?
tap to flip
A VM image is typically 5-20 GB (because it includes a full OS). A container image is typically 10-500 MB (because it includes only the app and its files). Big difference in shipping, storing, starting.
Why do containers start so much faster?
tap to flip
Because there's no OS to boot. Starting a container is starting a process on an already-running OS — milliseconds. Starting a VM means booting an OS — minutes.
VMs vs containers — isolation strength?
tap to flip
VMs give stronger isolation: each VM has its own kernel, so a kernel exploit in one can't reach the others. Containers share the host kernel — lighter, but a kernel bug affects all containers on the host.
Are VMs and containers competitors?
tap to flip
Mostly complementary. In the cloud, your "Kubernetes nodes" are usually VMs (provided by the cloud), and Kubernetes runs containers on top of them. So both are used together.
