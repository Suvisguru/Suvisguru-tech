Lesson 05 · should you actually adopt it?

When Kubernetes Fits — and When It's Overkill

Kubernetes is a power tool. Amazing for the right job, ridiculous for the wrong one. This lesson is about reading the situation honestly.

🚨 The 3 AM Nightmare

You adopted Kubernetes "because it's the modern way." A year in, two of your four engineers are full-time on cluster ops: upgrades, observability, security patches, certificate rotation, on-call. You're shipping fewer features than you would have on Heroku. Your investors are asking why. This lesson is about reading the situation honestly before adoption — not after.

🎯 If you remember nothing else: Kubernetes pays back at operational scale (many services, many teams). For a single small app, it's an industrial kitchen for your morning toast.

Section 1 · Concept

When Kubernetes pays off, when it doesn't

Kubernetes is enormously powerful — and that power has a real operational cost. The cluster itself is a small project to keep healthy: upgrades, observability, security patches, on-call. That cost is fixed regardless of how big or small your workloads are.

Kubernetes pays back when: you run many services across many teams, your workloads are dynamic (scale up and down, get rescheduled), you need workload portability across clouds and environments, and you have the engineering capacity to operate the cluster itself.

Kubernetes is overkill when: you have a single small application that rarely changes, no platform engineering capacity, latency-sensitive sub-millisecond workloads, or your real pain is application architecture not orchestration. Moving a bad app to Kubernetes makes it a bad app on Kubernetes.

The honest summary: Kubernetes solves operational scale and heterogeneity. If you don't have either, you don't need it — and adopting it costs you more than it gives you.

Section 1.5 · The fit-checker

Six questions to ask before adopting

Run through these on your own situation. Three or more "yes" answers and Kubernetes is likely a strong fit. Three or more "no" and you should look at simpler alternatives first.

1. Will you run 5+ services within 12 months?

YES → fit growsK8s pays back in proportion to service count.
NO → managed app platformRender, Fly, Heroku, ECS Fargate handle this fine.

2. Do 2+ teams share infrastructure?

YES → strong fitNamespaces + RBAC give clean isolation.
NO → simpler toolsSingle-team workloads don't need the multi-tenancy story.

3. Do workloads scale up & down dynamically?

YES → great fitHPA/VPA + cluster autoscaler shine here.
NO → not a strong driverSteady-state workloads don't need autoscaling.

4. Do you need workload portability?

YES → big advantageSame manifests run on EKS, AKS, GKE, on-prem.
NO → cloud-native of one cloud is fineApp Runner, Cloud Run, Azure Container Apps work great single-cloud.

5. Do you have platform engineering capacity?

YES → adopt confidentlySomeone owns cluster ops, upgrades, observability.
NO → DON'T adopt yetCluster ops without staff = burnout + outages.

6. Are workloads sub-millisecond latency-sensitive?

YES → consider alternativesK8s networking adds per-hop µs. HFT/RTB pay this cost.
NO → no concernFor 50ms HTTP APIs, K8s overhead is invisible.

All six "yes" → adopt confidently. Three or fewer → simpler tools first.

Three engineers, one Django app, one Postgres, deploying twice a week. Does Kubernetes fit?

Answer: b. Kubernetes pays back when many services and many teams are the bottleneck. With one app and three engineers, the cluster itself becomes the bottleneck.

Section 2 · Before & After

Same company, two moments. Same answer changes.

The same startup at two stages. At the start, Kubernetes would be ridiculous. Two years later, with growth, it becomes the obvious choice. The decision isn't permanent — it depends on where you are.

Same company, two years apart. Tooling that's wrong at year 1 is right at year 3.
Before · year 1

3 engineers, 1 service, 1 database, deploys via shell script. Total operational time: 2 hours per week. Adding Kubernetes here would add 10 hours per week (cluster maintenance, training, debugging, on-call) for zero benefit.

Right answer: managed app platform (Render, Fly, Heroku) or a single VM with systemd. Move fast, defer infrastructure complexity.

After · year 3

40 engineers, 8 services, 4 teams each wanting independent deploys, 3 environments (dev/staging/prod), traffic that scales 10x daily. Now ad-hoc tooling is the bottleneck — every team reinvents deploys differently.

Right answer: Kubernetes (managed: EKS/AKS/GKE) plus a small platform team. The cluster cost pays for itself in the operational standardization it provides.

What didn't change: Kubernetes never makes one service simpler. It pays off only when the count of things to operate becomes the bottleneck.

Section 3 · Analogy

Industrial kitchen vs the toaster on your counter

Imagine you walk into a professional industrial kitchen. Six chefs, twelve burners, four ovens, walk-in fridges, prep stations, plating stations, an expediter calling tickets, a head chef coordinating, a dishwashing crew, ventilation, refrigeration, fire suppression, written service standards. When you're feeding 800 people for a wedding, you need this. Every piece of that complexity is doing real work.

Now imagine you're making yourself toast in the morning. You need a toaster and a counter. If someone built you an industrial kitchen for your toast, the kitchen would consume more attention than the toast itself. The kitchen isn't bad — it's wrong for the use case.

The question isn't "is Kubernetes good?" Of course it's good. The question is "are you in the wedding-catering situation, or the morning-toast situation?" Adopt Kubernetes when the workload demands it, not because the technology is in vogue.

Section 4 · Two-level explanation

Two ways to hear it

Same idea, two depths.

ELI5 · explain like I'm 5

A big restaurant kitchen with many ovens and many cooks is amazing if you're feeding hundreds of people. It's silly if you're making one piece of toast. Kubernetes is the big kitchen. Sometimes you need it; sometimes you don't.

ELI10 · explain like I'm 10

Kubernetes is a platform — software for running lots of services across many machines, with deployment, scaling, restarts, secrets, networking, observability all standardized. When you have lots of services and multiple teams, that standardization is gold; you don't want every team inventing their own deploy script. When you have one service, the platform itself becomes work — you spend time keeping the cluster healthy instead of building product. A useful test: count the services you'll deploy and the teams that own them. Below ~5 services and ~2 teams, simpler tools usually win. Above that, the per-service cost of Kubernetes drops sharply.

⏸ Pause and check

A high-frequency trading firm has a trading engine that needs sub-100µs latency. K8s networking adds 50–200µs per Pod hop. Should the trading engine run on Kubernetes?

Answer: b (and also c). The trading engine goes on bare metal. The dashboards (normal HTTP latency) can happily sit on K8s. Different workloads, different platforms.

Section 5 · Real-world scenarios

Four real situations · clear answers

Each of these is a real-shape company with a real fit-or-not-fit answer for Kubernetes.

Mid-size media company · 40 services · 6 teamsCLEAR FIT

Spent 5 years on a custom Capistrano deploy from 2014. Each team's deploys broke differently; oncall debugging was a nightmare. Migrated to managed Kubernetes (EKS) over 6 months: collapsed 40 deploy scripts into one Helm pattern, replaced 6 staging setups with namespaced previews, freed two engineers from plumbing work. Six months in, deploys took 80% less time and platform engineers shifted to developer-experience work. The investment paid back inside one year.

4-engineer fintech · 1 core service + 1 workerWRONG FIT

Adopted Kubernetes "because it's the modern way." Spent a year on cluster ops: upgrades, observability, security patches, certificate rotation. Shipped fewer features than they would have on simpler tools. Eventually moved to ECS Fargate (managed container service) and kept Kubernetes only for the one service that actually justified it. The platform tax wasn't earning them anything; they recovered velocity in ~3 months.

Government agency · citizen portal · compliance-heavyFIT

Needed strict isolation between dev/staging/prod and an audit trail of every change. Kubernetes gave them per-environment clusters, RBAC for who can touch what, GitOps so every change has a Git commit, and policy enforcement via admission controllers. Compliance audits went from a 1-week scramble to "send the auditor a link to the Git repo." The structured platform was worth more than its operational cost.

High-frequency trading firm · sub-millisecond pathsWRONG FIT

Their core trading engine needs sub-100µs latency end-to-end. Kubernetes' networking adds 50-200µs per Pod hop (CNI plugin, kube-proxy, possibly service mesh). Manageable for normal HTTP traffic; lethal for HFT. They run their core engine on bare metal with kernel-bypass networking (DPDK), and use Kubernetes only for non-latency-critical services (dashboards, batch analysis, monitoring). Right tool for each job.

Section 7 · Misconceptions, flashcards & quiz

Lock it in

Three common misconceptions to clear up first, then the flashcards and quiz.

Common Misconceptions

Myth: Kubernetes makes one app simpler.
Truth: It never does. It pays off when the count of things you're operating becomes the bottleneck.
Myth: Managed Kubernetes is the same operational burden as self-managed.
Truth: Managed (EKS / AKS / GKE) takes the control plane off your plate — that's most of the ongoing pain.
Myth: If we don't pick Kubernetes now, we'll regret it later.
Truth: Most teams are better off starting on a managed app platform and migrating if they outgrow it. Migrations are real but cheaper than two engineers full-time on cluster ops at year one.
Three signals that Kubernetes is the right fit?
tap to flip
Many services to operate, multiple teams sharing infrastructure, dynamic workloads that scale and reschedule, need for workload portability across environments, existing operational maturity to run a cluster.
Three signals Kubernetes is overkill?
tap to flip
Single application that rarely changes, no platform engineering capacity, latency-sensitive sub-millisecond workloads, the team's current pain is application architecture rather than orchestration.
What is the platform tax?
tap to flip
The ongoing cost of running the cluster itself: upgrades, observability stack, security patches, on-call. Real number — for tiny workloads can exceed the value delivered; for big ones it amortises across many services.
What's a paved road?
tap to flip
The standard, supported way the platform team has decided things should be done. Easier than the alternative on purpose. A small team can't afford 10 ways to do everything.
When are managed services a better fit?
tap to flip
For small teams with few services. Render, Fly, Heroku, Railway — push code, get an HTTPS URL. You give up some control; you save weeks of operational work. Often the right call below ~5 services and 2 teams.
When does serverless win?
tap to flip
For spiky/event-driven workloads. AWS Lambda, Cloud Functions, Azure Functions. Pay per invocation; no servers to manage. Loses for steady-state long-running services where idle time is rare.
What are latency-sensitive workloads?
tap to flip
Workloads where sub-millisecond per-hop latency matters — high-frequency trading, real-time bidding, certain telecom paths. K8s networking adds microseconds; negligible at 50ms but expensive at 50µs.
Why doesn't moving 1 small app to K8s help?
tap to flip
K8s solves the count problem (running many things), not the per-app complexity problem. A single app on K8s pays the cluster operational cost without getting the benefit; on a single VM, you'd skip the cost entirely.

A 6-engineer team has 1 Django app, 1 Postgres, 2 background workers, 1 Redis. They're debating Kubernetes. What questions should drive the decision?

answerHow many services in 12 months? How many independent teams own deploys? Workload portability needed? Time for cluster ops? If answers are mostly "stays small," "one team," "no portability," "no platform time" — they should not adopt yet. If at least three answers point toward growth, adopt. Generally for this size: stick with managed app platforms or a small ECS/Cloud Run setup.

"We want Kubernetes because it's the modern way." Why is this a poor reason to adopt it?

answerAdoption isn't free — the cluster itself becomes ongoing work. K8s pays back in proportion to operational scale (many services, many teams). For workloads without that scale, the operational cost exceeds the value delivered. Adopt because the workload shape demands it, not because the technology is in vogue. Otherwise you're building an industrial kitchen for your toast.

Words from this lesson · open if you want a quick reference
Operational scale
The count of services to run, teams to support, and environments to maintain. Distinct from traffic scale; what K8s pays back on.
Platform tax
The ongoing cost of running the platform itself — upgrades, observability stack, security patches, on-call. Constant regardless of workload size.
Paved road
The standard, supported way the platform team has decided things should be done. Easier than the alternative on purpose.
Managed service
Render, Fly, Heroku, App Runner, Cloud Run — platforms where you push code and get an HTTPS URL. Often the right choice for small teams.
Serverless / FaaS
Functions that run only when invoked. AWS Lambda, Cloud Functions, Azure Functions. Wins for spiky/event-driven; loses for steady-state.
Latency-sensitive workload
Workloads where sub-millisecond per-hop latency matters — HFT, real-time bidding, telecom paths.
Managed Kubernetes
EKS (AWS), AKS (Azure), GKE (Google). Cloud provider runs the control plane; you run workloads. Significantly easier than self-managed.
Bare metal
Apps running directly on physical servers (no VM, no container). Used when latency or throughput requires it.
