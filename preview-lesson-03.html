Words you'll meet in this lesson

vSphere brings real product names to the concepts from lesson 1-2. Here's each one in plain language.

VMware tool
What it is: The company. Founded in 1998. Made x86 server virtualization mainstream and remains the dominant commercial platform.
When you hear "VMware shop," people mean an organisation running VMware's products. Different from other vendors (Microsoft, Red Hat, Citrix).
vSphere tool
What it is: VMware's flagship server-virtualization platform. The whole stack: ESXi + vCenter + the surrounding features (vMotion, HA, DRS, etc.).
"We run on VMware" almost always means "we run vSphere." This entire course is about understanding vSphere.
ESXi tool
What it is: The hypervisor itself. Bare-metal software installed on each physical server. Detailed in lesson 8.
Every host in a vSphere environment runs ESXi. It's the slicing layer that creates and runs VMs.
vCenter tool
What it is: The central management plane. Manages many ESXi hosts as one fleet. Detailed in lesson 9.
Without vCenter, each host is an island managed via the Host Client. With vCenter, hosts become one cluster — and you unlock vMotion, HA, DRS.
VM concept
What it is: Virtual Machine. A software container that looks to its OS like a real computer. The unit of compute work in vSphere.
VMs are what you actually run apps on. ESXi creates them; vCenter manages them; you deploy them.
Datastore concept
What it is: The logical storage container vSphere uses. Holds VM files (.vmdk, .vmx, snapshots, ISOs). Detailed in lesson 11.
Where VMs live. Every VM is stored on a datastore — VMFS, NFS, vSAN, or vVols flavoured.
Virtual network concept
What it is: Networking inside ESXi — vSwitches, port groups, vmkernel adapters. The plumbing that VMs and ESXi services use to talk. Detailed in lesson 10.
VM networking is fully virtualized. Hosts and VMs all plug into virtual switches that connect via physical NICs to the real world.
vSphere Client tool
What it is: The web UI vCenter serves at https://<vc-fqdn>/ui/. Where you live day-to-day.
When people say "log in to vSphere," they mean this. One tab, the whole environment.
Cluster concept
What it is: A group of ESXi hosts vCenter manages as a single resource pool. The unit DRS, HA, and shared storage are scoped to.
"Cluster" is where the fleet behavior lives. You don't think much about individual hosts — you think about cluster total capacity.
vMotion concept
What it is: Live migration of a running VM between hosts with zero downtime. The signature vSphere feature.
Lets hardware maintenance happen without scheduling app downtime. Touched here; covered fully in lessons 9 and 12.
HA · High Availability concept
What it is: Auto-restart of VMs on other hosts when their host dies. Different from vCenter HA (which protects vCenter itself).
Hardware failure becomes a 2-3 minute blip instead of manual rescue.
DRS · Distributed Resource Scheduler concept
What it is: Auto-load-balancing of VMs across hosts in a cluster. vCenter watches load and vMotions VMs around to even things out.
Set-and-forget capacity management. Detailed in lesson 9 for vCenter context.

Section 1 · Concept

Five named components

Lesson 1 introduced the hypervisor and the VM as concepts. Lesson 2 named five benefits. This lesson puts real product names on the things the previous lessons talked about — and adds the management plane that ties them together.

VMware is the company. Founded in 1998. Made x86 server virtualization mainstream and remains the dominant commercial virtualization platform.

vSphere is VMware's flagship product — a complete server virtualization platform. When practitioners say "we run on VMware," they almost always mean vSphere.

vSphere is built from five components:

  • Component 1
    ESXiESXi is VMware's bare-metal hypervisor — the software that runs directly on a physical server and creates the VMs. Every host in a vSphere environment runs ESXi.

    The hypervisor that runs directly on each physical server. From Lesson 1: ESXi is the apartment-building superintendent.

  • Component 2
    vCenter ServervCenter Server is the management plane that oversees many ESXi hosts at once. It provisions VMs, moves them between hosts, monitors health, and rolls out patches. One vCenter typically manages dozens to hundreds of hosts.

    The management plane. One vCenter inventories every host, every VM, every datastore — and lets the team operate them as one fleet.

  • Component 3
    Virtual machines

    The workloads. Same VMs as Lessons 1 and 2 — operating systems and applications running inside hypervisor-provided slices.

  • Component 4
    DatastoresA datastore is the storage abstraction where a VM's disks live. Underneath, a datastore can be a physical disk, a SAN volume, an NFS share, or a vSAN cluster — but to the VM, it just looks like storage.

    The storage abstraction. Where VM disks live. Underneath could be SAN, NFS, vSAN; the VM doesn't have to know.

  • Component 5
    Virtual networks

    The network abstraction — virtual switches and port groups inside each host. They connect VMs to each other and out to the physical network.

Every later VMware feature builds on these five components. Snapshots, vMotion, HA, DRS — all of them touch vCenter, ESXi, VMs, datastores, and networks in some combination.

Section 2 · Before and after

Fifty hosts, with and without vCenter

The same team running 50 ESXi hosts. The only difference is whether vCenter is in the picture.

Without vCenter

Fifty hosts, fifty silos

  • Each host configured by hand.
  • Moving a VM between hosts: power off, export, import, power on. Downtime each move.
  • Patching: SSH into each host one at a time.
  • Inventory is whatever's in a spreadsheet.
  • No single view of the environment.

With vCenter

One brain, fifty hands

  • vCenter inventories all 50 hosts.
  • Move a VM: click "migrate," pick destination — live, no downtime.
  • Patch a cluster: vCenter rolls through hosts in sequence.
  • Single inventory, single dashboard, single source of truth.
  • Every later feature (HA, DRS, vMotion) plugs in here.
Honest caveat

vCenter is now critical infrastructure. If it goes down, you can still run — every ESXi host keeps its VMs alive — but you can't manage until it comes back.

Section 3 · The analogy, extended

The property management company

The apartment building from Lessons 1 and 2 has a superintendent — that's the hypervisor (ESXi). One building, one super.

Now imagine a city with hundreds of buildings, each with its own super. Without coordination, every building is on its own.

Then a property management company takes over.

The company doesn't replace the supers — each super still runs their own building. But the company keeps a single inventory of every building, every apartment, every tenant. The company is the one place every building reports to.

Section 4 · At your level

The same idea, two depths

VMware is a company. They make a thing called vSphere that lets one big computer act like lots of small ones. vSphere has five parts: the part that lives on each computer (ESXi), the part that watches over all the computers from one place (vCenter), the pretend computers themselves (VMs), the place where all the pretend computers keep their files (datastores), and the wires that connect everything together (virtual networks).

VMware is the software company; vSphere is its platform for server virtualization. vSphere is built from five components.

ESXi is the hypervisor — Lesson 1's superintendent — installed on every physical host. It creates and runs the VMs locally. vCenter Server is the management plane that talks to every ESXi host in your environment; one vCenter typically manages tens to hundreds of hosts. Virtual machines are the workloads. Datastores are storage abstractions — the place VM disks live, regardless of the underlying physical storage. Virtual networks — primarily vSwitches and port groups — are the network abstraction.

Why this structure? Because at scale, managing each host individually is impossible. vCenter is the single brain that turns a fleet of hypervisors into one operational system. The next set of lessons (high availability, vMotion, DRS) all depend on vCenter being there.
