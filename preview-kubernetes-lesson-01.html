Lesson 01 · the start

What is Kubernetes?

It's the software that runs your apps across many computers — and keeps them running, automatically. Let's see what that means.

Section 1 · Concept

What Kubernetes actually is

Kubernetes (often shortened to K8s — the "8" is for the eight letters between K and s) is software that runs and manages your applications across many computers at the same time. You give it a description of what you want — for example, "I want three copies of my web app running" — and Kubernetes figures out which computers to run them on, watches them, restarts them if they crash, and adds more copies when you ask for them.

It runs on top of a group of computers called a cluster. A cluster can be three computers in a closet, or thousands of machines spread across data centres. Kubernetes treats them as one big pool of resources and decides where each piece of your app should live.

It exists because modern apps stopped fitting on a single computer. Companies need to run hundreds of services across many machines, with traffic that goes up and down through the day, with hardware that occasionally breaks. Doing all that by hand is impossible. Kubernetes does it automatically.

You hand Kubernetes a description. It figures out where everything should run, places it, and keeps it running.
Section 2 · Before & After

Life before Kubernetes vs life with it

Before Kubernetes, running an app at any real scale was a long list of manual steps. After Kubernetes, most of those steps disappeared — and the ones that remain are written down once, in a file.

Same time of night. Same problem. Different outcome.
Before

You SSH into the broken server. Read the logs by candlelight. Restart things by hand. Hope you didn't break something else. Document what you did so the next person can find it. Lose your Saturday.

Scaling means buying a bigger server, copying files, configuring it again, writing a runbook. Adding a new app means picking a server, hoping it has capacity, and praying.

After

You wrote down — once, in a file — what you want running. When something breaks, Kubernetes notices in seconds and starts a replacement on a healthy machine. You get a "self-healed" message. You stay asleep.

Scaling is changing one number in that file. Adding a new app is one more file. Kubernetes places it for you, watches it, and tells you when something needs your attention.

What didn't get easier: the app itself. Kubernetes won't fix bugs in your code. It just makes the operations around your code automatic.

Section 3 · Analogy

Kubernetes is like a property manager

You own a few apartment buildings and you don't want to manage them yourself. So you hire a property manager. You tell the manager what you want — "keep all the apartments rented to good tenants, fix anything that breaks, hire a plumber if there's a leak, find a new tenant if one moves out." And then you go live your life. You check in once in a while.

The manager handles the day-to-day: showing apartments, signing leases, calling repair people, replacing broken appliances, balancing tenants across buildings, keeping the lights on at night. You don't think about which plumber to call at 3 AM. You don't even know there was a leak.

Kubernetes is the property manager for your apps and computers. You tell it what you want. It handles the day-to-day.

You hire the manager. The manager handles the apartments. You sleep better.

The mapping:

  • You = the engineering team that owns an app
  • The property manager = Kubernetes
  • Apartment buildings = computers (servers / VMs / cloud machines)
  • Tenants = your apps (each one needs an apartment)
  • The instructions you gave the manager = your YAML files
  • The plumber the manager calls at 3 AM = Kubernetes restarting a crashed app
Section 4 · Two-level explanation

Two ways to hear it

Pick the level that fits where you are right now. They both say the same thing — one is just a little richer.

ELI5 · explain like I'm 5

Imagine you have a lot of toys, and a lot of toy boxes. A friend (Kubernetes) puts each toy in the right box, makes sure they all stay there, and brings out a new toy if one breaks. You don't have to remember which toy is in which box, or check on them. The friend just does it.

ELI10 · explain like I'm 10

Kubernetes is like a delivery dispatcher for a fleet of trucks. You tell it what packages need to go where; it picks which truck takes each one. If a truck breaks down, it sends another truck to finish the job. If demand goes up, it brings in more trucks. The dispatcher never sleeps. In Kubernetes, "trucks" are computers, "packages" are your apps, and "the dispatcher" is Kubernetes itself.

Section 5 · Real-world scenarios

Where Kubernetes shows up in real companies

Some concrete examples of teams running Kubernetes today, and what problem it solved for them.

A streaming media company (~200 engineers)

Runs hundreds of small services that together stream video to millions of viewers. Before Kubernetes, deploying a new version of any one service required a coordinated change across dozens of servers. Now each team writes a small YAML file and merges it; Kubernetes rolls the change out, watches for problems, and rolls back if the new version misbehaves. Average deploy time went from "an afternoon" to "twelve minutes."

A regional hospital system (~30 engineers)

Runs the patient records system, the pharmacy app, and a dozen integrations with lab equipment. The trade-off: they get the auto-recovery and consistent deploys they need, but they had to invest in a small platform team to keep the cluster healthy. After 18 months, downtime is down ~70% and adding a new app takes a day instead of a sprint.

A fintech startup (12 engineers)

Initially tried to run everything in Kubernetes from day one, "because it's the modern way." They spent more time fixing the cluster than shipping features. After a year they moved most workloads to a managed app platform (Render, Fly) and kept Kubernetes only for the one large service that justified it. Lesson: Kubernetes pays back when you have many services and many teams. With three engineers and one app, it's overkill.

A government agency running a citizen portal

Needed strict isolation between environments (dev, staging, production) and an audit trail of every change. Kubernetes gave them per-environment clusters, RBAC for who can touch what, and Git-tracked deploys. Compliance audits went from a one-week scramble to "send the auditors a link to the Git repo."

Section 7 · Flashcards & quiz

Lock it in

Eight flashcards to remember the key terms, then three quick check questions.

What is Kubernetes in one sentence?
tap to flip
Software that runs your apps across many computers and keeps them running automatically. You describe what you want; it figures out how.
Why is it called K8s?
tap to flip
Because there are 8 letters between the K and the s in "Kubernetes." It's a numeronym — same trick as i18n (internationalization) and a11y (accessibility).
What is a cluster?
tap to flip
A group of computers (anywhere from 3 to thousands) that Kubernetes treats as one big pool. Your apps run somewhere on the pool — Kubernetes picks where.
What does it mean to "describe what you want"?
tap to flip
You write a small file (usually YAML) that says things like "3 copies of my web app, listening on port 80." You don't say how to make it happen; Kubernetes works that out.
What does "self-heal" mean?
tap to flip
If an app crashes or a server dies, Kubernetes notices in seconds and starts a replacement on a healthy server. No human needs to wake up.
What problem does Kubernetes solve?
tap to flip
Running many apps across many computers, with auto-recovery, easy scaling, and consistent deploys — without doing it all by hand. It exists because modern apps stopped fitting on a single machine.
When is Kubernetes overkill?
tap to flip
For a single small app with a small team. The cluster itself becomes work to maintain. Kubernetes pays back when you have many services and many teams sharing one platform.
Who made Kubernetes?
tap to flip
Engineers at Google open-sourced it in 2014, based on Google's internal system called Borg. It's now governed by the Cloud Native Computing Foundation (CNCF) and developed by hundreds of contributors worldwide.
⏸ pause the animation

In the animation, the app moves from your request, into the Kubernetes wheel, then onto one of the servers. Why does the wheel get to pick which server?

answerBecause that's literally Kubernetes' job. You said "run my app" — you didn't say where. Kubernetes looks at all the servers, sees which ones have capacity, and picks one. This is called "scheduling." It's the reason you don't have to keep track of which app is on which server.

Your team has one small app with three users a day. A friend recommends you "move to Kubernetes." Should you?

answerAlmost certainly not. Kubernetes pays back when you have many services and the operations cost of managing them by hand becomes the bottleneck. With one tiny app, that bottleneck doesn't exist — you'd be paying the cost of running a cluster (a cluster is a small project to keep healthy) without getting any benefit. A managed app platform like Render or Fly is a better fit.

Why does describing what you want (instead of writing the steps) matter so much?

answerBecause the world keeps changing — servers fail, traffic spikes, new versions ship. If your instructions were a list of steps ("first do A, then B, then C"), they'd break the moment reality drifted from the plan. But if you described what you want ("3 copies running, healthy"), Kubernetes can keep adjusting reality to match — forever. The next lesson goes deep on this.
