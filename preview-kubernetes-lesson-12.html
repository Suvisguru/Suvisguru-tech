Section 1 · Concept

The shutdown protocol nobody told you about

When a Pod terminates, the kubelet doesn't just yank the plug. It runs a four-step protocol that depends on whoever is running as process 1 inside your container doing the right thing. Most "my Pod takes 30 seconds to die" mysteries trace back to PID 1.

Why PID 1 is special. Linux gives PID 1 (the first process started in a namespace) two responsibilities the kernel won't take care of for anyone else: (1) forward signals to its descendants when it gets them, and (2) reap zombies — call waitpid() on every child that exits, so the kernel can free the process table entry. On a normal Linux host, systemd does this. Inside a container, your ENTRYPOINT process is PID 1 — and most apps don't know they're supposed to do those jobs.

Signals K8s sends. When a Pod is deleted, the kubelet sends SIGTERM to PID 1 — the polite "please wrap up." If PID 1 hasn't exited within terminationGracePeriodSeconds (default 30s), the kubelet sends SIGKILL — the kernel-level "die now," uncatchable.

Graceful shutdown. A well-behaved app catches SIGTERM, stops accepting new work, finishes in-flight requests, flushes buffers, closes connections, exits 0. Pod status moves Running → Terminating → Terminated.

Init wrappers. tini and dumb-init are tiny binaries that solve both PID-1 problems. ENTRYPOINT ["tini", "--", "your-app"]: tini becomes PID 1, your app is PID 2. tini forwards every signal to PID 2 and reaps any orphan zombie that gets re-parented to it. One line, two big problems gone.

preStop hook. A Pod-level escape hatch that runs before SIGTERM is sent. Common pattern: preStop: sleep 10 — gives the load-balancer time to notice the Pod is being removed before connections stop. Critical for zero-downtime rolling updates.

Section 1.7 · Pod lifecycle phases

The five phases a Pod walks through

The Pod's status.phase is a high-level summary. Container-level state (Waiting, Running, Terminated) lives in status.containerStatuses[].

Pending → Running → Succeeded / Failed / Unknown
Pending: scheduled but not yet running (image pulling, init containers running).
Running: at least one container started; Pod is bound to a node.
Succeeded: all containers exited 0; restartPolicy will not restart.
Failed: at least one container exited non-zero; restartPolicy will not restart.
Unknown: kubelet can't reach the node — usually a network partition.

Container state changes more frequently than Pod phase: a Running Pod can have a Waiting container that's restarting after a crash.

Section 1.8 · Lifecycle hooks

postStart and preStop

Two hook points. Both run inside the container. Both can be exec or HTTP. Use them sparingly — most apps shouldn't need either.

postStart

Fires after the container is created, asynchronously to the entrypoint. No guarantee it runs before the entrypoint.

Use for: registering with a service mesh, warming a cache. Avoid: anything the app actually depends on (use init containers instead).

preStop

Fires before the kubelet sends SIGTERM. Blocks SIGTERM until preStop finishes (or grace period expires).

Use for: sleep 10 to let the load-balancer drain, kill -SIGUSR1 to trigger a graceful flush, draining Kafka consumers.

preStop is the most underused trick in K8s. A 10-second sleep here eliminates the LB-drain race in most rolling updates.

Section 1.9 · terminationGracePeriodSeconds

How long does the kubelet wait?

Wall-clock seconds between "preStop completes / SIGTERM sent" and SIGKILL. Default is 30. Tune it.

30s · default

Right for stateless web apps that drain quickly. Too short for: databases, queue workers in the middle of a long task, big buffer flushes.

120–600s · tuned

For databases (let WAL flush), batch workers (let current task finish), apps with large in-memory buffers. terminationGracePeriodSeconds: 300 is common for stateful workloads.

If the kubelet is impatient (e.g., during node drain), you can also be SIGKILL'd before grace expires. Don't rely on grace being honored under all conditions — make shutdown idempotent.

⏸ Pause and check

Your container's Dockerfile has CMD ["bash", "start.sh"] and start.sh runs node server.js &. K8s sends SIGTERM. What happens?

Answer: b. Bash is PID 1 and doesn't forward signals to background children. Node never learns the Pod is shutting down. After 30s grace, SIGKILL. In-flight requests dropped.

Section 2 · Before & After

Naive shutdown vs proper shutdown

Same Node app. Same signal arrives. Two different shutdowns — one drops requests, one doesn't.

Same SIGTERM arrives. The naive shutdown wastes 30 seconds and drops requests; the proper one finishes in under 8 seconds with zero loss.
Before · bash as PID 1

Dockerfile: CMD ["bash", "/app/start.sh"]. start.sh runs node server.js & in the background. SIGTERM arrives → bash receives it but doesn't forward to Node → Node never gets the signal → 30 seconds elapse → SIGKILL → Node dies mid-request, in-flight requests drop, no flush. Logs show "Killed". Operators wonder why every deploy is "lossy."

After · tini + preStop

Dockerfile: ENTRYPOINT ["/usr/bin/tini", "--"] + CMD ["node", "server.js"]. Pod adds lifecycle.preStop.exec.command: ["sh","-c","sleep 5"] and terminationGracePeriodSeconds: 60. preStop runs first, then tini forwards SIGTERM to Node, Node closes Express server gracefully, exits 0. Zero requests dropped.

What didn't change: the app code (assuming Node already handles SIGTERM, which Express does with one line). What changed: PID 1 actually delivers the signal.

Section 3 · Analogy

The ship's captain

A container shutdown is a ship docking and disembarking. The captain is PID 1. The crew (child processes) are at their stations: engine room, cargo hold, navigation. Out at sea, the lighthouse flashes a signal — that's SIGTERM from the kubelet — meaning "wrap up and head to port."

A good captain hears the signal, picks up the megaphone, and forwards the order to every crew member: "stop loading, wrap up the manifest, prepare to disembark." A bad captain hears the lighthouse and just stands there. The crew never gets the message and keeps loading cargo.

The captain also keeps a roster log — every crew member who disembarks gets ticked off (zombie reaping). Crew members whose original supervisor has already left get re-assigned to the captain (orphans), and the captain ticks them off when they leave too.

After the lighthouse signals, the harbour gives the ship a grace period (terminationGracePeriodSeconds, default 30s) to disembark peacefully. If the ship is still moored after grace, the harbour pulls the gangway and lights out (SIGKILL — can't be ignored). A captain who doesn't act in time loses crew mid-disembarkation.

⚠️ The analogy stops here: a real captain can refuse to leave port. PID 1 cannot refuse SIGKILL — it's uncatchable. The only way to leave on your own terms is to leave during the grace period.

Section 4 · Two-level explanation

Two ways to hear it

ELI5 · explain like I'm 5

A captain on a ship. The lighthouse blinks "time to leave." The captain picks up the megaphone and tells everyone. The crew packs up. The captain checks every name on a list as they leave. Then lights out. If the captain ignores the lighthouse, the harbour just turns out the lights anyway and people get stuck mid-task.

ELI10 · explain like I'm 10

Inside a container, the first process you start is PID 1 and gets two special jobs Linux normally hands to systemd: forward signals to descendants, and reap zombie children. When K8s wants to delete a Pod, it runs the optional preStop hook, then sends SIGTERM to PID 1. PID 1 forwards that to every child and waits for them to exit. If PID 1 doesn't exit within terminationGracePeriodSeconds (default 30), K8s sends SIGKILL — uncatchable. If PID 1 is bash, it swallows SIGTERM and your app never learns the Pod is shutting down. Wrap your entrypoint in tini (ENTRYPOINT ["tini", "--", "node", "server.js"]) — it forwards signals + reaps zombies for free. Add a preStop: sleep 5 if you need the load-balancer to drain.

⏸ Pause and check

During rolling updates, a small fraction of requests return 502 for a few seconds. Pods are healthy. The app handles SIGTERM correctly. What's the cause?

Answer: b. The endpoints update is slower than the app's shutdown. Fix: add a preStop hook that sleeps 10s before SIGTERM is sent — gives the LB time to drain the Pod from rotation.

Section 5 · Real-world scenarios

Where PID 1 quietly wrecks things

Bash-as-PID-1 dropped SIGTERM — every deploy was lossy

A team's deploys consistently took the full 30 seconds and ended with logs showing "Killed". Investigation: Dockerfile used CMD bash startup.sh and startup.sh ran node server.js & in the background. Bash was PID 1, caught SIGTERM, didn't forward it. Node never knew. Fix: ENTRYPOINT ["tini", "--"] + CMD ["node", "server.js"]. Deploys became instant and graceful.

30-second grace was too short — DB shutdown got truncated

A team's PostgreSQL sidecar was getting SIGKILL'd mid-checkpoint during Pod shutdowns. Result: occasional WAL corruption requiring recovery. Fix: terminationGracePeriodSeconds: 300 for that Pod, plus a preStop hook running pg_ctl stop -m smart. The DB gets up to 5 minutes to flush cleanly. Corruption stopped.

preStop sleep saved zero-downtime rolling updates

During rolling updates, a fraction of requests returned 502. Cause: Pod was shutting down before the Service's endpoint controller had updated the load-balancer; the LB kept sending traffic to a closed port. Fix: preStop: { exec: { command: ["sh", "-c", "sleep 10"] } }. Pod sits idle for 10s after being marked for deletion, giving the LB time to remove it from rotation. 502s dropped to zero.

Zombie process accumulation crashed a Java app under load

A Java app spawned subprocesses for thumbnail generation. Each finished quickly, but the JVM didn't reap them. Zombies accumulated until the process table filled, then "Resource temporarily unavailable" errors. Pod restart cleared it temporarily. Real fix: ENTRYPOINT ["tini", "--", "java", "-jar", "app.jar"]. tini reaped zombies as they appeared. Crashes stopped.

Section 7 · Misconceptions, flashcards & quiz

Lock it in

Three common misconceptions to clear up first, then the flashcards and quiz.

Common Misconceptions

Myth: SIGKILL is "just a stronger SIGTERM."
Truth: SIGKILL is uncatchable, kernel-level. Your app cannot clean up under SIGKILL — it just dies. That's why graceful shutdown matters.
Myth: terminationGracePeriodSeconds is a hint.
Truth: It's a hard wall-clock timer. SIGKILL fires at the end. Tune it (60s, 300s) for slow shutdowns — databases, queue workers, big flushes.
Myth: Zombies are theoretical.
Truth: A Java app spawning subprocesses without waitpid() will fill the PID table over hours. Real outage. tini solves it for free.
Why is PID 1 special inside a container?
tap to flip
Linux gives PID 1 two jobs the kernel won't do for anyone else: forward signals to descendants, and reap zombie children (waitpid). On a host, systemd does this. In a container, your ENTRYPOINT process is PID 1.
SIGTERM vs SIGKILL — when does K8s send each?
tap to flip
On Pod deletion, kubelet sends SIGTERM to PID 1 (polite "wrap up"). If PID 1 hasn't exited within terminationGracePeriodSeconds (default 30s), kubelet sends SIGKILL — uncatchable.
What does tini do?
tap to flip
Tiny init binary. ENTRYPOINT ["tini", "--", "your-app"]. tini becomes PID 1, app is PID 2. tini forwards every signal to PID 2 and reaps any zombie re-parented to it. Solves both PID-1 problems.
What is a zombie process?
tap to flip
A process that has exited but whose entry remains in the process table because the parent hasn't called waitpid(). Zombies don't use CPU or RAM but consume PID slots. Accumulating zombies fill the table.
What is preStop and what's it for?
tap to flip
Pod hook that runs BEFORE SIGTERM is sent. Common pattern: preStop sleep 10 — gives the load-balancer time to notice the Pod is being removed before connections stop. Saves rolling updates from 502s.
terminationGracePeriodSeconds — what is it?
tap to flip
Seconds the kubelet waits between sending SIGTERM and SIGKILL. Default 30. Tune higher (120-600+) for slow shutdowns: databases, queue workers, big flushes.
Why is CMD ["bash", "start.sh"] dangerous as entrypoint?
tap to flip
bash becomes PID 1, catches SIGTERM but doesn't forward it to spawned children. App never learns the Pod is shutting down → SIGKILL → dropped requests. Use tini or exec the app directly.
Five Pod phases?
tap to flip
Pending (scheduled, not running yet), Running (at least one container started), Succeeded (all containers exited 0), Failed (at least one container exited non-zero), Unknown (kubelet can't reach node).

Your team's deploys consistently take exactly 30 seconds and end with "Killed" in logs. Dockerfile has CMD ["bash", "/app/start.sh"] and start.sh runs node server.js &. Why slow and lossy, and what's the fix?

answerbash is PID 1 inside the container. SIGTERM arrives, bash receives it, but bash does NOT forward signals to its background children. Node never learns the Pod is shutting down. Kubelet waits the full 30s grace, then SIGKILL — uncatchable, drops in-flight requests. Two fixes: (1) Use tini as PID 1: ENTRYPOINT ["tini", "--"], CMD ["node", "server.js"]. tini forwards SIGTERM to Node and reaps zombies. (2) Or skip the wrapper: ENTRYPOINT ["node", "server.js"] — Node becomes PID 1 and gets SIGTERM directly. Make sure Node has a SIGTERM handler that closes the HTTP server gracefully.

During rolling updates, a small fraction of requests return 502s for a few seconds. Pods are healthy. The app handles SIGTERM correctly and shuts down in under a second. Cause and fix?

answerRace condition between Pod termination and load-balancer endpoint removal. When K8s marks a Pod for deletion, two things start in parallel: kubelet sends SIGTERM (and your app shuts down), AND the endpoints controller updates the Service to remove the Pod from the LB. The endpoints update is slower than your shutdown. So briefly the LB is still sending traffic to a Pod whose port has already closed → 502. Fix: add a preStop hook that sleeps for ~10 seconds before the app receives SIGTERM. lifecycle: { preStop: { exec: { command: ["sh", "-c", "sleep 10"] } } }. The sleep blocks SIGTERM until the LB has had time to drain the Pod from rotation.
